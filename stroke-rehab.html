The Gap in Stroke Rehab

Why Most Stroke Rehab
Falls Short

The reality of stroke recovery in the U.S. is this: most patients receive acute rehabilitation in the hospital, some post-acute therapy, and then — if insurance stops covering it — they're expected to maintain or progress on their own. But the work is far from finished.

"The brain needs high-intensity, high-repetition, task-specific practice to rewire after stroke. Most traditional rehab doesn't get there."

Standard home health and outpatient PT programs are often limited in intensity, environment, and creativity. They focus on what's safe, not what's hard. They teach compensation strategies rather than pursuing recovery of the affected side. They prevent falls rather than teaching you how to get back up from one.

At STRIVE, we believe the plateau most stroke survivors experience is not a ceiling — it's where the real work begins. Our sessions are structured around the evidence: high-intensity repetition, task-specific practice, and progressive challenge drive neuroplasticity long after the acute phase.

Our Philosophy

Steps Towards Recovery

These aren't add-ons or bonuses — they are the core of how we approach stroke rehabilitation differently from every standard program in Las Vegas.

Fall Recovery Training

We don't just help you avoid falls — we train you to get back up. Floor-to-stand recovery is a high-priority functional skill that most programs skip entirely. We know patients often doubt they can do it. We know they can. We prove it, step by step, with full support until it's automatic.

Functional Confidence

Heavy Work & Weight Training

Heavy pushing, heavy pulling, and progressive resistance training on the affected side. Not compensatory work for the strong side — deliberate loading on the arm or leg that the stroke affected. The brain needs effort and resistance to rebuild motor programs. We do not shy away from hard work.

Neuroplasticity Driver

Obstacle Courses

Real environments are not flat. They have curbs, thresholds, rugs, uneven terrain, and narrow passages. We create structured obstacle courses — indoors or outdoors — that simulate these challenges and build the adaptability that transfers directly to your daily life in Las Vegas.

Environmental Challenge

Agility Drills & Reaction Time

Stroke impairs reaction time, a key predictor of fall risk and functional independence. We use agility ladder drills, perturbation training, and reactive stepping exercises that sharpen the nervous system's ability to respond to the unexpected — something standard balance exercises do not train.

Nervous System Training

Reactive Balance Training

Perturbation-based training — controlled, unexpected challenges to your balance — has a higher evidence base for fall prevention than static balance exercises alone. We introduce controlled instability, reactive stepping, and dual-task balance challenges that prepare you for what the real world will throw at you.

Evidence-Based

Community Reintegration

We practice where life happens. Grocery stores. Parking lots. Restaurants. Outdoor sidewalks and curb cuts in real Las Vegas neighborhoods. Community reintegration is a clinical priority, not an afterthought — because independence is the goal, not just safe ambulation on a tiled clinic floor.

The Science of Intensity

Why High-Intensity Training
Changes Stroke Recovery

For decades, the assumption in stroke rehabilitation was that patients are too fragile for vigorous exercise. Current evidence has overturned that assumption decisively. High-intensity interval training (HIIT) is now recognized as one of the most potent drivers of neuroplasticity after stroke.

High-intensity aerobic exercise increases brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) — the primary molecular signal that promotes synaptic plasticity and motor learning. Moderate-intensity exercise does not reliably produce this effect.

Consistent finding across neuroplasticity and stroke rehabilitation literature

HIIT after stroke is not about pushing to exhaustion — it is about reaching a training intensity zone that activates the neurochemical cascade that the brain needs to rewire. At STRIVE, our physical therapists apply evidence-based HIIT protocols adapted to each patient's current cardiovascular fitness, neurological status, and functional capacity — with continuous clinical monitoring throughout every session.

The result: faster motor recovery, greater cardiorespiratory fitness, reduced fatigue, and — critically — improvements in gait speed and balance that exceed what low-to-moderate intensity programs produce.

Increased BDNF Production High-intensity aerobic exercise reliably elevates brain-derived neurotrophic factor — the key driver of synaptic plasticity and motor re-learning after stroke.
Faster Gait Speed Recovery HIIT protocols show significantly greater improvements in 10-meter walk test and 6-minute walk test performance compared to standard moderate-intensity stroke rehab.
Improved Cardiovascular Health Stroke survivors have substantially elevated cardiovascular risk. HIIT addresses both motor recovery and the underlying cardiac health that determines long-term independence.
Clinically Safe — When PT-Supervised Evidence consistently shows HIIT is safe for stroke survivors under appropriate clinical supervision. Adverse events in PT-supervised HIIT trials are extremely rare and comparable to moderate-intensity programs.
Reduced Post-Stroke Fatigue Paradoxically, high-intensity training reduces chronic post-stroke fatigue over time — a finding that moderate-intensity programs do not consistently replicate.
Life-Saving Skill

Fall Recovery Training:
Learning to Get Back Up

Here is an uncomfortable truth about most stroke rehabilitation programs: they teach patients to avoid falling but never teach them what to do when it happens. Falls are a reality of life after stroke. Up to 73% of stroke survivors fall within the first six months of returning home. Fear of not being able to get up is one of the most significant contributors to activity avoidance and deconditioning.

"Teaching a patient to get up from the floor is not a luxury — it is a core functional skill. The fear of not being able to get up is itself disabling."

We know that patients often deeply doubt their ability to get up from the floor after stroke. We know that this doubt is powerful and often prevents people from even attempting it. At STRIVE, we approach fall recovery training with full structure, full support, and complete step-by-step progression — so that by the time patients attempt it independently, they've already done it dozens of times.

This is not a one-time session. It is a skill trained, reinforced, and progressed like any other functional goal — until it is automatic, confident, and independent.

1
Assessment & Fear Acknowledgment We assess your current floor transfer ability, fear level, and physical barriers so the plan starts where you actually are.
2
Controlled Floor Descent Before we teach getting up, we practice getting down in a controlled, stepwise way to build confidence with floor contact.
3
Supported Floor-to-Stand Progression Using mat, chair, or therapist support, we train the stages of floor-to-stand with hands-on guidance and repetition.
4
Progressive Independence Support is gradually reduced as skill and confidence improve, helping the task feel safer and more automatic over time.
Common Questions

Stroke Rehabilitation FAQs

Is high-intensity training safe after stroke? +
Yes — when supervised by a qualified physical therapist. Multiple randomized controlled trials and systematic reviews have confirmed that HIIT is safe for stroke survivors with appropriate screening and monitoring. Our doctoral-level physical therapists monitor your cardiovascular response, exertion level, and neurological signs throughout every session. We screen carefully and progress conservatively, but we do not use "caution" as a reason to keep you at an intensity that won't drive recovery.
How is this different from outpatient PT or home health? +
Standard outpatient and home health PT are often limited by time, setting, and intensity. Most outpatient programs work at moderate intensity, in a clinical environment, with a therapist managing multiple patients. Home health is constrained to what's safe in a small space. At STRIVE, every session is 1-on-1, private, and built around progressive challenge — including heavy resistance work, obstacle courses, agility drills, and community access that simply aren't possible in those settings.
My stroke was over a year ago. Is it too late to make gains? +
No. The idea that recovery stops after six months is outdated. While the rate of spontaneous neurological recovery slows after the acute phase, motor learning — driven by practice, intensity, and task-specificity — continues throughout life. Many of our patients make meaningful gains years after their stroke, particularly in strength, gait speed, community independence, and fall confidence. The key is applying the right stimulus at the right intensity.
Do you accept insurance? +
STRIVE is currently a private-pay practice for stroke rehabilitation sessions. This allows us to provide the level of individual attention, session intensity, and clinical creativity that insurance-based models make structurally difficult. We can provide documentation for out-of-network reimbursement upon request. Contact us to discuss payment options and packages.
What does a typical session look like? +
Sessions are fully individualized — no two are identical. A session might include a HIIT aerobic warm-up, progressive resistance training of the affected extremities, task-specific functional practice (such as stair climbing or object manipulation), reactive balance or agility drills, and cool-down with stretching and breath work. Fall recovery training is integrated as a regular component for appropriate patients. Community sessions take place in real Las Vegas environments.
