Why Home Matters

Why Traditional Clinics Can Be a Bad Fit for Vestibular Patients

Many people with vestibular dysfunction are told to "just get to therapy," but that advice misses the reality of the condition. The trip itself can be part of the problem. Car motion, visual flow through the windows, rapid head turns, parking lots, busy lobbies, and fluorescent clinic spaces can all spike symptoms before treatment even starts.

When a patient arrives already overwhelmed, it becomes harder to get accurate baseline information and harder to dose exercises the right way. That can make therapy feel discouraging instead of empowering.

"Your first vestibular session should not begin with a symptom flare caused by the commute."

At STRIVE, the environment becomes part of the treatment plan. We start with what is actually hard for you, in the setting where those challenges show up, then progress carefully and intentionally from there.

What We Address

Vestibular Rehab for Common Dizziness Presentations

The exact plan depends on your evaluation, but vestibular physical therapy may help patients dealing with positional vertigo, motion sensitivity, imbalance, gaze instability, and other symptoms related to vestibular dysfunction.

BPPV / Positional Vertigo

Assessment for positional dizziness and vertigo, including treatment when canalith repositioning maneuvers are appropriate.

Chronic Dizziness

Progressive exercise and movement strategies for persistent dizziness, disequilibrium, and reduced activity tolerance.

Visual Motion Sensitivity

Care for patients who feel worse in busy visual environments, on screens, in stores, or with fast-moving surroundings.

Vestibular Hypofunction

Gaze stabilization, balance training, and walking progression for vestibular loss and related functional limitations.

Balance and Fall Risk

Targeted practice for turning, uneven surfaces, transitions, and confidence with everyday movement at home and in the community.

Post-Concussion Vestibular Symptoms

For patients managing dizziness, head-motion intolerance, and visual-vestibular symptoms after concussion or mild brain injury.
